The most meaningful professional conversations do not announce themselves. They arrive quietly, in the middle of ordinary London afternoons, between two people who simply agreed to be present for thirty minutes. What follows is one of those.

The Encounter

Some of the most meaningful professional conversations do not start with a pitch deck. They start with curiosity.

What was meant to be a thirty-minute introduction turned into something far more valuable: a genuine exchange between two people building, learning, and trying to solve real problems in the world.

What stayed with me was not just what Patrick is building through Otters, but why he is building it.

Patrick is not someone who looked at a market from a distance and decided there might be an opportunity there. He has lived the problem. He has seen, first-hand, where partnerships break down, where good intentions get lost in process, and where businesses struggle because the systems around collaboration are not built for how people actually work.

That matters. Because when someone has carried the weight of a problem themselves, the solution they build carries a different kind of honesty.

That is what stood out to me about him. His work with Otters is not just a business proposition. It is a passion-driven response to something he understands deeply. He saw a challenge in his earlier career, took ownership of it in his own mind, and then decided to turn that lived experience into something useful — so that other companies and startups do not have to face the same friction.

Not noise. Not hype. Not "AI for the sake of AI." Lived pain, turned into clarity, turned into action.

That, to me, is one of the best kinds of founder energy.

On Conviction

"When someone has carried the weight of a problem themselves, the solution they build carries a different kind of honesty. You can feel it in the room. You can feel it in the product."

The conversation had a particular quality that is increasingly rare. There was no performance. No positioning. Two people genuinely interested in each other's work, asking real questions, sitting inside the answers without rushing to the next agenda item.

London does this to you sometimes. The grey light, the pace of a great city, the fact that everyone here is building something — it creates a particular kind of permission to be honest with strangers.

Then the conversation took another turn that made it even more interesting. We started talking about vibe coding.

I shared how I built VaxGuard — a live app that came from a real-world need around vaccination tracking, where too much still depends on paper, memory, and disconnected records. Using Codex and Vercel changed the game for me entirely.

As someone who is not a traditional developer by practice, being able to think through a feature, build it, test it, improve it, and keep moving — without endless cycles of explanation and handover — has been profoundly powerful.

Common Ground

You can feel an idea in the morning and begin shaping it the same day. You can test conviction much faster. You can move from "I think this could work" to "let me show you" — without waiting for perfect conditions. That part of the conversation was energising, because it was not theoretical. It was two people, from different professional lanes, seeing the same thing.

On Building in the Real World

Patrick spoke about his journey from Austria to Munich to London, now building a life here with his wife and kids. That resonated. I have my own journey, my own family, my own children — and there is something grounding about speaking with people who are ambitious, creative, and building seriously, while also being deeply anchored in family life.

That combination matters. Because ambition without anchoring is just noise. The most interesting builders I know are also the most present people in their actual lives. They are not escaping into work. They are building from something.

We also touched on advisory work, startup ideas, travel, and the kind of problems worth solving. I shared some of the work I have been doing across retail, travel, hospitality and B2B — along with advisory conversations I have been having with founders and operators. We found overlap in travel-tech thinking, where a simple conversation opened up reflections on itinerary-led experiences, calendars, AI, and how technology might become more genuinely useful in everyday life.

That is the other thing about conversations like this. They do not stay in one lane. They wander, pleasurably, into places you had not expected to visit. That wandering is not inefficiency. It is exactly where the interesting ideas live.

The Lesson

We often talk about networking as if it is transactional. Too often it is reduced to outreach, positioning, follow-ups, and pipelines.

But conversations like this are a reminder that real networking — when done properly — is something far more powerful. It is about meeting like-minded people. It is about recognising passion. It is about exchanging lived experience. It is about seeing how someone else is approaching a problem and leaving the conversation sharper, more energised, and more open than before.

In just thirty minutes, I did not just "meet someone new."

I got a deeper view into a founder's conviction.

I saw how a real problem had become a product.

I heard how new tools are changing what founders can build.

And I was reminded that meaningful conversations still matter — perhaps more than ever, in a world that is becoming increasingly digital.

So this is also a note to myself. Keep making time for conversations like this. Keep connecting with builders. Keep learning from people solving real problems. Keep exchanging ideas with generosity. Because sometimes one honest conversation can do more than weeks of passive scrolling ever will.

Patrick, thank you for the conversation, the openness, and the energy. You are building from a place of lived understanding. And that always shows. That is the kind of work worth paying attention to.
